Early Life and Career

Barabara Liskov (née Huberman) was born November 7, 1929 in Los Angeles, California. She earned a Bachelor's degree in mathematics with a minor in Physics from UC Berkeley in 1961, where she was the only female student in her class. After being denied admission to Princeton University's graduate program in mathematics on the basis of her gender, she worked for research labs and universities in Boston, MA for several years before enrolling in Stanford University's graduate program in Computer Science. In 1968, she earned her Ph.D., becoming one of the first women in her field to earn a doctoral degree. She wrote her doctoral dissertation on an AI for chess endgames, and was responsible for developing the 'killer heuristic', a way to help an AI identify strong moves more efficiently.

Turing Award

In 2008, Dr. Liskov won the ACM Turing Award, the so-called Nobel Prize of computing, for her "contributions to practical and theoretical foundations of programming language and system design, especially related to data abstraction, fault tolerance, and distributed computing." Her contributions to modern programming langages cannot be overstated, and she is truly one of the most important figures in the field of software engineering.
